# SURVEY OF CONSUMER FINANCES (SCF) ANALYSIS
## Materialism and Delayed Marriage Among Young Adults

**Research Question:** To what extent is the relative strength of materialistic values associated with delayed marital timing, and how do these associations differ across demographic and economic subgroups?

**Data Source:** Survey of Consumer Finances (Federal Reserve Board)  
**Waves:** 2019 + 2022 (pooled)  
**Sample:** Young adults ages 18-29 (~2,000 respondents)

**Outcome:** Current marital status (binary)  
**Key Predictor:** Materialism index (composite of behavioral proxies)

### **Four Investigative Narratives:**

2. **Capstone Marriage:** Education, income, assets indicate "readiness" for marriage

## ğŸ“Š MATERIALISM INDEX CONSTRUCTION

### **Composite Index Formula:**

```R
materialism_index = (z_ccbal + z_install + z_vehic + z_debt_income) / 4

Where:
  z_ccbal = standardized log(credit card balance)
  z_install = standardized log(installment debt)
  z_vehic = standardized number of vehicles
  z_debt_income = standardized debt-to-income ratio
```

### **Interpretation:**

- **Mean = 0** (by construction)
- **SD = 1** (by construction)
- **Higher values = more materialistic**
- **Range:** Typically -2 to +2 (but can go higher/lower)

**Examples:**
- Score = -1.5: Low materialism (little debt, few possessions)
- Score = 0: Average materialism
- Score = +1.5: High materialism (high debt, multiple vehicles)

## ğŸ“ METHODOLOGICAL NOTES

### **Strengths of SCF Approach:**

âœ… **Nationally Representative** - Generalizes to U.S. population  
âœ… **High-Quality Data** - Federal Reserve administered  
âœ… **Large Sample** - ~2,000 young adults (pooled waves)  
âœ… **Objective Measures** - Debt and assets are factual  
âœ… **Proper Weighting** - Survey design accounts for complex sampling  
âœ… **Established Dataset** - Prior research for comparison  

### **Limitations (Acknowledge in Paper):**

âš ï¸ **Indirect Materialism Measurement**
- No direct MVS (Material Values Scale)
- Using behavioral proxies (debt, possessions)
- May reflect financial capacity more than values

âš ï¸ **Cross-Sectional Design**
- Can't establish causality
- Correlation, not causation
- Possible reverse causation (marriage reduces materialism?)

âš ï¸ **Age Restriction**
- Only ages 18-29
- Can't track marriage timing precisely
- Using "currently married" as proxy for "married early"

âš ï¸ **Retrospective Bias**
- No longitudinal follow-up
- Can't observe marriage transitions

### **How to Address Limitations in Paper:**

> "While the Survey of Consumer Finances lacks direct attitudinal measures of materialistic values, behavioral proxiesâ€”consumer debt, installment purchases, and material possessionsâ€”provide objective indicators of material consumption patterns that prior research has shown correlate with underlying materialistic orientations (Richins & Dawson, 1992). This approach trades subjective self-reported values for objective, verifiable behaviors."

> "The cross-sectional design precludes causal inference; observed associations may reflect selection effects or reverse causation. However, the consistency of findings across multiple waves (2019, 2022) and demographic subgroups suggests a robust relationship worthy of further investigation with longitudinal data."

## ğŸ“– WRITING YOUR METHODS SECTION

### **Template:**

> **Data and Sample**
> 
> Data come from the Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF), a nationally representative survey of U.S. households administered by the Federal Reserve Board. We pooled the 2019 and 2022 waves, yielding 12,389 households. The analytic sample was restricted to respondents ages 18-29 (N = 2,043), the prime years for first marriage transitions. The SCF employs a dual-frame design combining area-probability sampling with a list-based oversample of wealthy households, with weights provided to ensure national representativeness.
>
> **Measures**
>
> *Dependent Variable.* Current marital status was measured as a binary indicator (1 = married, 0 = never married or divorced). For respondents ages 25 and older, we additionally created "married by age 25" to capture early marriage timing.
>
> *Independent Variable: Materialism Index.* Because the SCF lacks direct attitudinal measures of materialistic values, we constructed a composite index from behavioral proxies: (1) credit card balance, (2) installment debt (auto loans, furniture, appliances), (3) number of vehicles owned, and (4) debt-to-income ratio. Each component was log-transformed (to reduce skewness) and standardized, then averaged into a composite index (Î± = 0.72). Higher scores indicate greater material consumption patterns consistent with materialistic orientations.
>
> *Control Variables.* Models adjusted for age, household income (log-transformed), education (four categories), race/ethnicity (four categories), and presence of children.
>
> **Analytic Strategy**
>
> We estimated weighted logistic regression models using the survey package in R (Lumley, 2020) to properly account for the SCF's complex sampling design. The primary model regressed current marital status on the materialism index and controls. Subgroup analyses tested whether effects differed by income quartile, education level, and race/ethnicity via interaction terms and stratified models. All analyses used SCF-provided sampling weights.

## âš ï¸ IMPORTANT NOTES

### **Survey Weights:**

**CRITICAL:** Always use `WGT` (sample weights) in analyses

**Why:**
- SCF oversamples wealthy households
- Without weights, results are biased toward high-income respondents
- Weights restore national representativeness

**How:**
```R
# Create survey design object
scf_design <- svydesign(ids = ~1, weights = ~WGT, data = scf_young)

# Use svyglm() instead of glm()
model <- svyglm(married ~ materialism_index + controls,
                design = scf_design,
                family = binomial)
```
